# Extracting Client DNA

## Overview

A repeatable method for extracting everything an AI needs to know about a business's ideal client — their pain, language, worldview, objections, and transformation — into a deployable profile. Generic AI content fails because it lacks this context. Client DNA fixes that.

**Output:** A Client DNA Profile (structured markdown) that any AI model can use alongside a Voice DNA Profile to generate marketing content that actually resonates — landing pages, emails, social posts, ad copy, funnel content.

## When to Use

- Defining the ideal client for a specific product or service
- Building the strategic foundation for a marketing funnel
- Preparing context so AI tools can generate targeted content
- Onboarding a new client into a content or marketing workflow
- Repositioning or launching a new offer

**Not for:** Generic demographic research, market sizing, competitor analysis, or product development. This is about depth on ONE ideal client for ONE specific product.

## The Core Principle

Every product has a different ideal client. This process maps ONE client for ONE product. If the business has multiple products, run this separately for each.

The completed profile should be so specific that an LLM reading it could:
1. Write a landing page that makes the ideal client feel seen
2. Draft an email sequence that addresses their real objections
3. Create social content using the client's own language
4. Build a funnel that follows their actual decision journey

## Process Overview

Five phases, conversational and deep. Each builds on the previous. Don't rush — thin answers produce generic content.

1. **Ground** — Establish the product and who it's for
2. **Map the Current State** — The client's world before the solution
3. **Map the Desired State** — What changes after
4. **Uncover the Gap** — Objections, false beliefs, and buying barriers
5. **Extract the Language** — How the client actually talks about all of this

Total time: ~45-75 minutes as an interactive conversation.

## How to Run This

This skill is designed as an **interactive conversation**. Don't hand the user a questionnaire to fill out alone — that produces surface-level answers. Instead, walk them through it section by section, asking follow-up questions to push past the obvious.

### Your role as interviewer

- Ask one section at a time. Don't dump all questions at once.
- When an answer is vague, push: "Can you give me a specific example?" / "What would they literally say about this?"
- Mirror their language back to confirm understanding.
- After each phase, summarize what you've captured before moving on.
- If they get stuck, offer examples or provocations to unstick them.

### Starting the conversation

Open with:

> "We're going to build a complete profile of your ideal client — not a demographic sketch, but the kind of deep understanding that lets AI generate content that actually resonates with real people.
>
> This works best as a conversation. I'll ask questions one section at a time, and I'll push for specifics where it matters. The more concrete and honest your answers, the better the output.
>
> Let's start: **What's the specific product or service we're building this profile for?**"

## Phase 1: Ground — The Product and the Person

Establish the basics before going deep. Get clear on:

1. **What's the product/service?** — In their own words, not marketing copy.
2. **Who is it for?** — Not demographics. Who is this person? What's their situation? What's true about their life right now?
3. **Why does this product exist?** — What did the business owner see that made them build this? What frustration or gap drove it?

### Push for specifics

- "When you say [target audience], describe one specific person you've worked with who was the perfect fit."
- "What was happening in their life when they found you?"
- "If you had to describe your ideal client to a friend over coffee, who are you describing?"

## Phase 2: Map the Current State — Their World Before You

This is the richest section. Take your time here.

### 2A: The Problems (What hurts)

1. **What are the 2-4 core problems your product solves?** Not features — the actual pain points in the client's life.
2. **What does each problem cost them?** — Time, money, energy, relationships, opportunities. Be specific.
3. **How long have they been dealing with this?** — Is this a new pain or a chronic one they've normalized?

Push: "If your ideal client was venting to a friend about this over drinks, what would they actually say?"

### 2B: The Mistakes (What they're doing wrong)

1. **What mistakes is the client making that keep the problem alive?**
2. **Do they know they're making these mistakes, or are they invisible to them?**
3. **What seems logical to them that's actually counterproductive?**

Push: "What's the thing they're doing that makes you want to grab them and say 'stop — that's making it worse'?"

### 2C: The Failed Solutions (What they've already tried)

1. **What has the client tried before to solve this?** — Specific products, services, approaches, DIY attempts.
2. **Why didn't those solutions work?** — Not "they were bad" — what specifically was missing or wrong?
3. **How do those failed attempts affect their willingness to try again?** — Are they skeptical? Burned? Desperate?

Push: "When they tell you what they've tried before, what's the thing that makes you think 'no wonder that didn't work'?"

### 2D: The False Belief (What they think they need)

This is positioning gold. The gap between what the client thinks they need and what they actually need is where all great marketing lives.

1. **What does the client believe is the solution to their problem?**
2. **Why is that belief wrong or incomplete?**
3. **According to YOUR approach, what do they actually need?**

Push: "What's the thing they'd Google to solve this? And why would that search lead them in the wrong direction?"

## Phase 3: Map the Desired State — Their World After You

### 3A: Practical Transformation

1. **What concretely changes in their life/business after working with you?**
2. **What can they do now that they couldn't before?**
3. **What does a typical day/week look like after vs. before?**

Push: "If I followed your ideal client with a camera for a week after they've gone through your program/used your product — what would I see that's different?"

### 3B: Emotional Transformation

1. **How do they feel differently?**
2. **What do they stop worrying about?**
3. **What becomes possible that felt impossible before?**
4. **How do the people around them notice the change?**

Push: "What's the message or call you get from a happy client that makes your whole week? What do they actually say?"

### 3C: The Identity Shift

1. **Who do they become?** — Not just what changes, but how they see themselves differently.
2. **What do they start saying or believing about themselves that they didn't before?**

Push: "Complete this sentence from the client's perspective: 'I used to be someone who... now I'm someone who...'"

## Phase 4: Uncover the Gap — Objections and Buying Barriers

### 4A: Self-Directed Objections ("It's not for me because...")

1. **Why does the ideal client think this won't work for them specifically?**
2. **What makes them feel like their situation is different or special?**
3. **What past failure are they projecting onto this?**

Push: "When someone who IS your ideal client says no — what reason do they give? Not the polite reason. The real one."

### 4B: Solution-Directed Objections ("This approach won't work because...")

1. **What doubts do they have about your method, product, or approach?**
2. **What have they heard or assumed that makes them hesitant?**
3. **What competitor or alternative are they comparing you to in their head?**

### 4C: Timing and Priority Objections

1. **Why not now?** — What makes them delay?
2. **What would have to happen for them to feel urgency?**
3. **What trigger moment finally makes them act?**

Push: "Think about someone who bought from you. What happened in the week or month before they reached out? What broke, changed, or clicked?"

## Phase 5: Extract the Language

This phase is what makes AI content feel real instead of generated.

### 5A: Client's Own Words

1. **How does the ideal client describe their problem in their own words?** — Not your professional terms. Their language.
2. **What phrases do they use repeatedly?** — In DMs, intake calls, comments, reviews.
3. **What do they search for on Google, YouTube, or social media?**

Push: "Open your DMs, intake forms, or testimonials right now. What words and phrases keep showing up? Read me a few actual messages."

### 5B: Emotional Language

1. **What words do they use when they're frustrated about this problem?**
2. **What words do they use when they describe what they want?**
3. **What metaphors or comparisons do they reach for?**

### 5C: Market Awareness Level

1. **How aware is the ideal client of their problem?** — Do they know they have it, or do you need to show them?
2. **How aware are they of your type of solution?** — Do they know solutions like yours exist?
3. **How aware are they of you specifically?**

This determines whether content should lead with pain (problem-aware), solution (solution-aware), or differentiation (product-aware).

## Building the Profile

After all five phases, synthesize everything into the Client DNA Profile format. See [client-dna-profile-template.md](client-dna-profile-template.md) for the full output structure.

### Quality Checks

- **Real language, not summaries.** If the profile says "clients feel overwhelmed," that's useless. "I literally can't keep up, something's going to fall through the cracks" is usable.
- **Specific, not generic.** Every claim should be traceable to something the business owner actually said during the conversation.
- **Tension is present.** The profile should capture contradictions — what clients say vs. what they do, what they want vs. what they need.
- **Actionable for AI.** An LLM reading this profile should be able to generate a landing page without asking follow-up questions.

### Common Issues

- Profile is too positive — no real objections or friction captured
- Language section is paraphrased instead of quoted
- Transformation is vague ("feel better") instead of specific
- Missing the false belief — this is the most important positioning element and the most commonly skipped

## Deployment

The completed Client DNA Profile is used alongside the Voice DNA Profile:

| Component | Purpose |
|-----------|---------|
| **Client DNA Profile** | WHO you're talking to, WHAT they need to hear, WHY they should care |
| **Voice DNA Profile** | HOW you say it — tone, structure, vocabulary |

Together, these give an LLM everything it needs to generate content that sounds like the business owner and resonates with their ideal client.

### Deployment targets

| Platform | Method |
|----------|--------|
| Claude Project | Upload as knowledge file alongside Voice DNA |
| Custom GPT | Include in instructions or knowledge base |
| API / automation | Include in system message or context |
| Content briefs | Reference when writing content strategy |

### Maintenance

- **When messaging doesn't land:** Revisit the language section — are you using your words or theirs?
- **When objections shift:** Update the barriers section. Markets evolve, objections change.
- **New product or offer:** Run the process again. Different product = different ideal client.

## Tips from Real Extractions

- **Intake forms and DMs are gold.** The language clients use before they buy is the most authentic source of copy.
- **The false belief is your biggest leverage point.** Content that names the wrong belief and offers the right one is the most engaging format across every platform.
- **Specificity beats persuasion.** "I help overwhelmed moms" loses to "I help moms who are running a business from their kitchen table while their toddler naps and wondering why their Instagram isn't converting." The detail IS the persuasion.
- **Trigger moments drive urgency.** Generic content says "you need this." Good content describes the exact moment when they realize they need this.
- **One ideal client, not three.** If the profile tries to capture multiple audiences, it captures none. Narrow ruthlessly.
- **Emotional transformation sells; practical transformation retains.** Lead with how they'll feel. Deliver on what concretely changes.
